\begin{theorem}
The number $\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that $\sqrt{5}$ is rational. Then there exist integers $a$ and $b$ such that $b \neq 0$, $\gcd(a,b)=1$, and
\[
\sqrt{5}=\frac{a}{b}.
\]
Since $b \neq 0$, we may square both sides to obtain
\[
5=\frac{a^2}{b^2}.
\]
Multiplying both sides by $b^2$ gives
\[
a^2=5b^2.
\]
Thus $5 \mid a^2$.

We claim that $5 \mid a$. Indeed, because $5$ is prime, Euclid's lemma implies that if $5 \mid a^2=a\cdot a$, then $5 \mid a$. Hence there exists an integer $k$ such that
\[
a=5k.
\]
Substituting this into $a^2=5b^2$ gives
\[
(5k)^2=5b^2,
\]
so
\[
25k^2=5b^2.
\]
Dividing both sides by $5$ yields
\[
5k^2=b^2.
\]
Therefore $5 \mid b^2$. Again, since $5$ is prime, Euclid's lemma implies that $5 \mid b$.

Thus $5 \mid a$ and $5 \mid b$, contradicting the assumption that $\gcd(a,b)=1$. Therefore our supposition was false, and $\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.
\end{proof}
